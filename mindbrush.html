Our Solution

MindBrush is an app that promotes mental hygiene by making aspects of it a daily routine.

My responsibilities included behaviorial research, strategy, and the development of the prototypical web application. Our goal was to create an app users could trust in their times of stress, sadness, and affliction.

Research

My team and I performed 100+ interviews.

Mental illness is highly stigmatized, and most solutions are prescriptive rather than proactive. Current mental health apps in the market have low retention rates.

Often times, messaging such as "Are you feeling depressed today" placed unnecessary stigmas on users.

Students were so overwhelmed with school, extracurriculars, and work, that they often found it difficult to make time for their support networks. They wished for more time to do the things that mattered to them.

We sought to create a solution that would automatically schedule self-care time for students, so that they could feel more at ease with themselves.

How it works

We prototyped a web app first before moving to a mobile app, in order to minimize risk and prove the concept quickly.

1. Students go through an initial onboarding.

Here, the web app walks the user through the basics and explains the process of onboarding.

2. Students list their interests and hobbies.

Here, the web app would use machine learning to suggest self-care activities for the student to do.

3. The app schedules self-care activities based on the students' interests.

The app would also automatically schedule friends with overlapping free time to do the same activities together.

4. Based on feedback, the app offers insights into the person's mental hygiene.

Here, the app uses machine learning to understand the student, offering key insights into how they function and behave.

Outcome

First prize in UVA's Social Entreprenuership Competition.

With two of my friends, we pitched Mindbrush to a crowd and panel of 3 judges and came away with 1st prize and $1000 in prize money in the Social Entreprenuership track.

Afterwards, we prototyped a series of initiatives to further test the app, before ultimately disbanding due to more pressing matters in school.

This concept app provided the basis for my mental health research with the Elliewood Fellowship. After disbanding, I was still very interested in the idea of mental health prevention and how it related to the college mental health system.

I spent the next 12 months fully unpacking the college mental health space - learning the ins and outs and doing behaviorial research with every single player.
